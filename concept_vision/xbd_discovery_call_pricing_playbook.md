## Part 1: Before The Call — Mindset

### Who You Are On This Call

You are **not** a freelancer on a job interview.

You are a **creative director** conducting a **diagnostic consultation**. You are the doctor. They are the patient. The patient doesn't tell the doctor what medicine to prescribe. The patient describes their symptoms, and the doctor — based on expertise — recommends the treatment.

This frame is everything. If you lose this frame, you lose the call.

### The Power Dynamic Is Already In Your Favor

Before the call even starts, remember what's true:

- They've seen the video. They've seen what you can do. **The proof already exists.**
- They reached out to YOU (or responded enthusiastically). That means desire is already established.
- You have something they want but don't have. You are the supply to their demand.
- You are not negotiating from zero. You are negotiating from a position of demonstrated capability.

### The One Rule

**You do not mention price until THEY bring it up, or until the very end of the call — whichever comes LAST.**

Price is the last conversation, never the first. If they bring it up early, you redirect:

> *"Great question — I want to give you an honest number, not a guess. Let me understand a few more things about what you actually need so I can be precise. Is that fair?"*

Nobody says no to that. You've just bought yourself 20 more minutes of diagnostic authority.

## Part 3: The Call Structure — 5 Phases

The call should be **30-45 minutes**, not 20. Here's why: 20 minutes is enough for a transactional conversation ("how much, can you do it, when"). You want a **transformational conversation** — one where they feel heard, where the gap becomes undeniable, and where you position as the only logical solution.

If they say "I only have 20 minutes," that's fine. You'll compress. But aim for 30-45.

---

### Phase 1: Rapport & Framing (3-5 minutes)

**Goal:** Set the frame. You are the consultant. This is a diagnostic, not a pitch.

**Opening (after pleasantries):**

> *"Thank you for making the time — I really appreciate it. Before we dive in, I want to set a quick expectation for how I like to work. I'm going to ask you quite a few questions — some of them might feel like they're going deep into areas you wouldn't expect from someone building a website. The reason is, I don't just build websites. I build digital experiences that are direct extensions of a brand's identity. And to do that well, I need to understand things most agencies never ask about. Is that okay with you?"*

**Why this works:**
- You've pre-framed the questions as a sign of expertise, not inexperience
- You've differentiated yourself from "most agencies"
- You've gotten permission to go deep — they can't object later when you ask probing questions
- This is exactly the Chris Do approach: set the frame early

**Then add:**

> *"Also — I want to be transparent. I'm not here to sell you anything today. I want to understand where you are, where you want to go, and whether what I do is actually the right fit. If it's not, I'll tell you. Fair?"*

This is non-attachment, verbalized. It disarms them completely.

---

### Phase 2: Business Context (7-10 minutes)

**Goal:** Understand the business, not the website. This is where you separate yourself from every other designer they've ever talked to.

**Questions to ask (not all — pick the ones that feel right in the moment):**

#### About the business:

> *"Can you tell me a bit about where XBD is right now as a studio? What's the current focus — is it more hospitality projects, private residential, or a mix?"*

> *"When you look at the next 12-18 months, what does growth look like for XBD? Are you trying to attract a specific type of client, or expand into a new market?"*

> *"Who is your ideal client right now? If you could pick your dream project, what does it look like?"*

#### About the competitive landscape:

> *"When a potential client — say, a developer with a waterfront site in Dubai — is choosing between XBD and another firm, what do you think makes the difference? What wins the project for you?"*

> *"Are there other studios whose digital presence you admire? Not necessarily competitors — just firms whose websites make you think 'they got it right'?"*

#### About the current situation:

> *"How is the current website performing for you? Is it generating the kind of inquiries you want, from the kind of clients you want?"*

> *"When a prospective client visits xbdesign.com today, what impression do you think they leave with?"*

> [!TIP]
> **Listen for pain.** When they answer these questions, they will reveal frustrations they might not even be aware of. "We get a lot of inquiries but they're not the right ones." "Our website doesn't really represent us." "We built it years ago and haven't had time to update it." Each of these is ammunition for later.

**Notes to take during this phase:**
- Revenue-related signals (number of projects per year, average project size)
- Pain points they verbalize
- Their language — use their exact words back to them later
- Who makes decisions (is it Ellen alone, or is there a business partner?)

---

### Phase 3: The Pain & The Gap (5-7 minutes)

**Goal:** Make them feel the gap — the same gap you identified in the brand diagnosis. But they need to say it, not you.

This is where belief-level selling happens (Level 3 from Jeremy Miner's framework). You're helping them confront a limiting belief: *"Our website is fine for now."*

**Questions:**

> *"You mentioned [something they said about their ideal client]. When that person lands on your website right now — do you think the experience they have matches the experience of walking into one of your completed spaces?"*

Let them sit with that. Don't fill the silence.

> *"If I showed your website to someone who's never heard of XBD, and then I showed them the Solar House photography — would they believe it's the same firm?"*

This is devastating. And it's not an attack — it's a question. They answer it themselves. And the answer is no.

> *"How important is the digital first impression to winning projects at the level you're operating at?"*

They will say "very important." Now they've admitted the problem AND its significance — out loud, to you.

**The reframe (if they minimize the gap):**

If they say something like "Well, we get most of our work through referrals anyway," respond:

> *"That's great — and it tells me the work speaks for itself, which it absolutely does. But let me ask you this: when someone gets referred to you — what's the first thing they do? They Google you. They visit the website. They look at the Instagram. That referral already did the hard work of creating trust — the question is whether the digital experience maintains it or leaks it."*

"Leaks" is the word. Premium leaks. They'll feel it.

---

### Phase 4: The Vision (5-7 minutes)

**Goal:** Paint the picture of what their digital presence SHOULD feel like. This is where the Loom video pays dividends — they've already seen it.

**Questions:**

> *"When you saw the homepage concept we built — what was your honest first reaction?"*

Listen carefully. Their answer tells you everything about how to position the proposal.

> *"If we could extend that same quality across your entire digital presence — project pages, the about section, the inquiry experience — what would that mean for XBD?"*

You're asking them to expand the scope. Not you. THEM. They're dreaming bigger now.

> *"If a potential client — someone considering a $5 million or $10 million project — could experience your work online the way they experience it in person, how would that change the quality of inquiries you receive?"*

This question does two things:
1. It introduces the dollar amounts of THEIR projects (we'll need these for anchoring)
2. It connects the website directly to revenue — it's not a cost, it's an investment

> *"What would it feel like to send a prospective client a link to your website and feel proud of it — to know it represents the best version of what you do?"*

Identity-level question. This hits at who they want to be, not what they want to buy.

### Phase 5: Scope & Pricing (7-10 minutes)

This is where the price conversation happens. **Only now.** After they've:
- Admitted the gap
- Described the pain
- Envisioned the solution
- Connected the website to revenue
- Expanded the scope themselves

#### Step 1: Summarize What You Heard

> *"Let me make sure I heard you right. You're a studio doing [X type of projects] at [Y scale]. Your ideal client is [Z]. Right now, the website isn't generating the quality of inquiries you deserve, and the digital experience doesn't match the caliber of the built work. What you want is a digital presence that makes the same statement as walking into one of your completed spaces. Did I get that right?"*

They say yes. Now they've confirmed the problem, the desire, and the gap — all in their own words, summarized back to them.

#### Step 2: The Chris Do Price Anchoring Sequence

This is the part you've been asking about. Here is the exact flow:

> *"Can I ask you something? What's the average project value for XBD? Like, when you take on a residential project — what's a typical engagement worth?"*

They might say $500K, $1M, $2M — whatever it is, it's a big number. Architecture/interior design firms at this level are charging serious fees.

Let's say they say **$500,000** (conservative for a firm doing Bvlgari-level work).

> *"And how many new projects do you typically take on per year?"*

Let's say they say **8-12 projects.**

> *"So the studio is generating roughly $4-6 million a year in project revenue? That's incredible. And right now, the digital presence — the first touchpoint for many of those clients — is a WordPress template from 2014 with a broken awards page. Does that feel proportional to you?"*

The contrast does the work. You don't need to say "you should invest more." The math speaks.

> *"Here's how I think about it. If a better digital experience helps you win even one additional project per year — or helps you attract a higher-caliber client who chooses you over a competitor — that's $500K in additional revenue. The investment in the website becomes a fraction of one project's value."*

Now you've reframed the website from a COST to an INVESTMENT. And you've anchored the value at $500K+.

#### Step 3: Present The Number

> *"Based on what you've described — and knowing the level of craft that goes into something like what you saw in the concept — a full cinematic website experience for XBD would be in the range of $25,000 to $45,000."*

Pause. Let it land. Don't speak.

If they react calmly → they expected it, or it's within range. Proceed to scope discussion.

If they react with surprise → it's fine. You anchored at $500K per project. Even $45K is less than 10% of one project's value. Remind them:

> *"I know that sounds significant. But think about it this way — that's less than 10% of a single project's revenue. And this isn't a brochure website. This is a cinematic digital experience that positions XBD the way the built work deserves. It's the difference between being seen as a regional firm and being seen as a global design authority. Which one attracts the $5M projects?"*

## Part 4: The Pricing Tiers

Here's what to actually charge, and why.

### Understanding The Market

| Service Level | What It Includes | Market Rate (Global) | Your Rate (Position) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Homepage Only** (what you've built) | Cinematic hero, scroll experience, project showcase section, inquiry section, mobile responsive | $8,000 - $20,000 | **$12,000 - $18,000** |
| **Core Website** (homepage + 4-5 inner pages) | Homepage + About/Studio + Projects (3-5 case studies) + Contact/Inquiry + Navigation system | $20,000 - $45,000 | **$25,000 - $35,000** |
| **Full Digital Experience** (everything) | Core Website + CMS integration + Brand guidelines implementation + SEO + Analytics + 3-month support | $40,000 - $80,000+ | **$40,000 - $55,000** |

### Why These Numbers

- XBD is a Dubai-based firm building for Bvlgari, Rixos, St. Regis. They are used to premium pricing. Charging $5K would make you look unserious.
- Design agencies building cinematic architecture websites (Basic Agency, Locomotive, Active Theory) charge $50K-$200K+ for this caliber of work.
- You are newer, so you're pricing below top agencies — but NOT at freelancer rates. You're in the "emerging studio" tier, which is exactly where you are.
- The concept you've already built would cost a client $8K-$15K from most studios. You gave it for free. That's leverage, not a loss.

### The Recommended Approach: Two Options, Not Three

Don't present three tiers. Present two. Here's why:

- Three options → people pick the middle (and you've anchored low)
- Two options → the conversation becomes "which scope," not "whether to hire you"

**Option A — The Homepage Experience: $15,000**
> What they've already seen, production-ready. Fully responsive. Deployed.

**Option B — The Full Website: $35,000**
> Homepage + Studio/About + 5 project case studies + Inquiry system + Mobile-first + CMS for future project updates.

**Present Option B as the recommendation:**

> *"Honestly, if it were my brand, I'd go with Option B. The homepage is the door — but what's behind it matters just as much. If a client lands on that cinematic homepage and then clicks into a project page that looks like the current site, the spell breaks. For the investment to pay off, the experience needs to be consistent all the way through."*

Now they're choosing between A and B, not between you and nobody.

## Part 5: Objections & How To Handle Them

### "That's more than we expected."

> *"I appreciate you being honest. Can I ask — what were you expecting? Because I'd rather understand your framework than assume."*

Listen. Then:

> *"Here's the thing — I can absolutely adjust the scope to fit a different number. But I won't adjust the quality. If we do a smaller scope, every page we do will be at the same level as what you saw. We just do fewer pages. What would be the most important pages for you?"*

You've just done the Chris Do "how can we get creative" move — reducing scope, not quality.

### "We need to think about it."

> *"Of course. This is a significant decision and I'd want you to feel completely right about it. Can I send you a summary of what we discussed, with the two options laid out? That way you have something concrete to review with your team."*

Then send a beautifully formatted proposal (not a PDF template — a clean, branded document) within 24 hours. Speed = seriousness.

### "Can you do it for less?"

> *"I won't do the work at a quality level I'm not proud of, and I know you wouldn't want that either. But here's what I can do — we can start with Option A, the homepage. Get that live. Let it work for you. And then when you're ready, we extend to the full site. That spreads the investment and lets you see results before committing to the full scope. Would that work?"*

You've maintained your price, offered a path forward, and introduced the idea of a phased engagement — which often ends up being worth MORE than the upfront quote.

### "We already have a web agency (E8)."

> *"I respect that — and I'm not here to replace anyone. What I'm offering is a different category of work. What you have now is a functional website. What I build is a cinematic brand experience. They solve different problems. If you're happy with the current direction, I completely understand. But if you've been feeling like the digital presence doesn't match the built work — that's the gap I close."*

### "Can you just give us the homepage you already built?"

> *"What you saw is a concept — a demonstration of direction. Turning it into a production-ready, responsive, optimized site with real content integration is a different scope of work. Think of it like an architectural rendering vs. a built space — the concept shows the vision, the production makes it real. That's where the investment goes."*

This is their own language. Rendering vs. built. They'll understand it immediately.

## Part 6: After The Call

### Within 24 hours: Send the proposal

A clean, well-designed document (Google Doc or Notion page, not a messy PDF) with:

1. **Recap** — "Here's what we discussed" (3-4 bullet points of THEIR pain, in THEIR words)
2. **The Vision** — "Here's where we're going" (2-3 sentences about the cinematic digital experience)
3. **Option A** — Scope, deliverables, timeline, price
4. **Option B (Recommended)** — Scope, deliverables, timeline, price
5. **Timeline** — "From kickoff to launch: 6-8 weeks"
6. **Next Steps** — "If you'd like to move forward, here's what happens first"

### Within 3 days: One follow-up

> *"Just checking in — wanted to make sure the proposal made sense and see if any questions came up. No rush at all."*

### After 7 days with no response: Final touch

> *"Hey — totally understand if the timing isn't right. I'll be publishing the case study this week and starting on my next project. If you ever want to revisit, the door's always open. Wishing XBD an incredible year."*

Walk away. For real. This is the hardest part and the most important part. Walking away IS the close. Because 60% of the time, they come back within 2-4 weeks.

You don't say this unless they push hard on price. But know it. Feel it. It's true.

## Part 9: What Chris Do Would Do

Since you asked directly — here's how Chris Do would run this call, distilled from everything in those reference files:

1. **He would NOT justify the price.** He'd present it as fact. "This is $35,000." Period. Not "this is $35,000 because..." The price is the price.

2. **He would anchor on THEIR value, not his cost.** "What's a project worth to you? $500K? This investment is less than 10% of one project."

3. **He would use the "how can we get creative" line if budget is tight.** "I can't do it for that number. But let's design something that gets you what you need within what you have."

4. **He would never compete on price.** If they say "we got a quote for $5K from someone else," he'd say: "Then you should hire them. If what you need is a $5K website, I'm not your person. If what you need is what you saw in that video — I am."

5. **He would frame it as a relationship, not a transaction.** "I'm not looking for a one-off project. I'm looking for a brand I believe in that I can grow with. XBD is that for me."

6. **He would walk away without flinching.** The willingness to lose the deal IS the thing that wins it. People can feel desperation. And they can feel freedom. Be the free one.
